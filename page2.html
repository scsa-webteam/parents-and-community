How will my child be assessed?

Assessment is used to determine student progress and achievement. Good assessment helps teachers know what teaching and learning strategies are working in their classes and where there are gaps in what students have learnt. Assessments give students, teachers and parents information about a student's progress in learning.

What types of assessment are there?

There are three main types of assessment and they relate to how the assessment is used.

Diagnostic

Diagnostic assessment is used to identify student strengths and weaknesses. It may be used before or during a learning cycle to pinpoint where to provide support.

Formative

Formative assessment is used to monitor student progress. It happens during a teaching and learning cycle. It can be used to give a student feedback about how well they have performed or what they need to do to improve. It can also help a teacher judge how much students have learnt and whether all students are able to show their ability.

Summative

Summative assessment is used to make a 'final' determination of student achievement. It usually comes at the end of teaching and learning cycle. The length of a teaching and earning cycle may vary. Summative assessment should also enable students to show their ability.

What are the guiding principles for assessment?

The Outline sets out principles for assessment. These set the scene for assessment and ensure it is useful and fair.

  1. Assessment should be an integral part of teaching and learning

    Assessment, teaching and learning should all come from the curriculum as it is set out in the syllabus. They need to be connected. Assessment should give feedback to teachers and students about the students' learning progress and inform a teacher's ongoing planning.

  2. Assessment should be educative

    Assessment should help students learn. Students should be a part of the assessment process. They should have opportunities to reflect on what they have learnt and to plan for how to improve.

  3. Assessment should be fair

    Assessment needs to take account of the needs of students. It needs to be valid. A valid assessment is one that assesses what it is supposed to assess. For example, recall of facts should not be assessed if the main purpose of the assessment is to collect information about problem-solving skills.

  4. Assessments should be designed to meet their specific purposes

    The purpose of the assessment should be clear. It should also be clear what students are expected to do.

    Formative assessment should be used to see what students have learnt. It should be used to show gaps in what students have learnt and to inform ongoing teaching. Summative assessment should be used for assessment of learning and come at an end-point.

  5. Assessment should lead to informative reporting

    Assessment should provide information about student achievement that can be used for reports. The reports should give feedback about a student's learning progress and what they need to do to improve. Reports are intended to be helpful for students, parents, teachers and schools.

  6. Assessment should lead to school-wide evaluation processes

    Assessment provides information that schools can use to plan and to meet student needs. Teachers reflect on student achievements from the present and the past to plan for improvements in future teaching, learning and assessment.

Achievement standards

Across Foundation to Year 10, achievement standards indicate the quality of learning students should typically demonstrate by a particular point in their schooling. Achievement standards comprise a written description and student work samples.

An achievement standard describes the quality of learning (the extent of knowledge, the depth of understanding and the sophistication of skills) that would indicate the student is well placed to commence the learning required at the next level of achievement.

The sequence of achievement standards across Foundation to Year 10 describes progress in the learning area. This sequence provides teachers with a framework of growth and development in the learning area.

Year 11 and 12

Every student studying a WACE course must be provided with the:

  • school’s senior secondary assessment policy
  • syllabus
  • school’s course outline
  • school’s assessment outline.
School assessment policy+
  • Each school is required to develop a senior secondary assessment policy which provides information to Year 11 and Year 12 students, their parents/guardians/carers and teachers on the following:
  • how the required curriculum and assessment documents will be provided to students
  • security of assessment tasks
  • rules for the conduct of school examinations and externally set tasks
  • assessment of students who do not complete the assessment program
  • procedures to be implemented if assessment is affected by a catastrophic event
  • assessment of students with special educational needs
  • reporting of student achievement
  • authentication of student work (including cheating, collusion, plagiarism, appropriation, etc.)
  • assessment of students transferring between courses and/or units
  • assessment of students transferring between schools
  • retention and disposal of student work
  • assessment review and appeal processes.

See section 2 of the WACE Manual – 2017 – web-updated

Assessment reviews and appeals+

The school is required to provide Year 12 students with the final school mark and grade submitted to the Authority and with the opportunity to query their school assessment and resolve any issues which may arise. See section 2.9 WACE Manual – 2017

ATAR Examinations+

ATAR course examinations are developed and conducted for all Year 12 ATAR courses.

The School Curriculum and Standards Authority sets, administers and marks the ATAR course examinations.

Students who are enrolled in a Year 12 ATAR course pair of units are required to sit the ATAR course examination. There are both written and practical examinations for some ATAR courses.

For more information see Section 6 of WACE Manual – 2017

What does assessment look like in a classroom?

Assessment is an ongoing process. It can be formal or informal. Teachers monitor students' learning progress in classroom activities as well as in projects, assignments and tests.

The form of an assessment may depend on the age of the student and the subject or learning area. It will also depend on the types of skills or understandings students are required to demonstrate.

A teacher may use many assessment tools and strategies. Some of these are listed below.

ACHIEVEMENT BOARD OR POSTER

a visual summary of learning and achievement that the student can make and add to over time.

ANECDOTAL NOTES

records of a student's achievements and challenges in class and homework activities that are kept by the teacher.

AUTHENTIC TASKS

activities that involve students in 'real life situations' where they can apply their learning in practical ways such as designing and making products to sell in a fundraising stall.

CONFERENCES AND CONVERSATIONS

collections of information about a student's progress through formal and informal discussions.

CONTRACTS

formalised goal setting that may include a timeframe along with a student's identified aims.

EXTENDED WRITING

tasks such as narratives, reports, persuasive texts and essays.

GAMES

participation in games (physical, language, mathematical, logical) to provide evidence of knowledge, understanding and skills.

LEARNING JOURNALS

students reflect on their progress, achievements and challenges. They may also set goals and plan for 'the next step' in their learning.

Observation checklist

a record of students’ having shown evidence of key skills and understandings.

Peer evaluation

feedback from other students about a student’s participation and contribution in activities.

Portfolios

a formal collection of student work, often with a reflective and planning element that is used to ‘showcase’ a student’s learning progress and achievements.

Projects

extended activities that may give students scope to design, research, build and question in order to develop a substantial piece of work.

Role play

students act out scenarios to show their understanding of processes, situations and ideas.

Rubrics

a grid that breaks down student performance into what teachers look for when marking how well the students performed. These observations link to the standards of achievement used in reporting.

Tests and quizzes

formal and informal questions to collect information about student knowledge, skills and understandings.

Work samples

a selection of a student work that can be used to show how one student’s work compares with another and how students are ranked across the ability range.
